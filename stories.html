Taxes & Finance March 2026
AI caught a tax error that would have cost me money

Tax season means a pile of documents — W-2s, a dozen 1099s, mortgage statements, rental income. This year I asked AI to read through all of them, reconcile the numbers, and cross-check against my tentative tax return. It found something I never would have caught on my own.

Result: Removing the 401(k) dividends from the return corrected the tax liability and reduced what was owed. More importantly, it prevented filing an inaccurate return — which creates a much bigger headache if the IRS ever asks questions.
What happened, step by step
1

I uploaded every tax document I had — W-2s from both employers, 1098 mortgage forms, and 1099s from every brokerage account. I asked AI to build a complete federal tax picture and flag anything unusual.

2

The AI worked through each file methodically — wages, dividends, capital gains, rental income, deductions. Along the way it noticed that an amended Wells Fargo 1099 sitting in my uploads had different numbers than the original version — a correction I would have missed entirely.

3

When I uploaded my tentative Form 1040 to double-check the numbers, the AI flagged a clear mismatch: the return was reporting substantially more dividend income than any of my 1099 documents could account for.

4

Tracking it down account by account, the AI identified the source: two Charles Schwab account summaries for accounts held inside a 401(k) workplace retirement plan. These are brokerage window accounts — the dividends they earn are tax-deferred and not reportable income on a tax return.

5

The AI pointed out that Schwab’s own document header explicitly stated: “The information in this report is not being reported to the IRS by Charles Schwab.” Tax software had imported the file anyway and silently counted those dividends as taxable income. The error would have gone straight to the IRS unflagged.

The takeaway: tax software imports whatever files you feed it. It can’t distinguish between a taxable brokerage 1099 and a 401(k) plan account summary. AI, reading the actual documents with full context, spotted the difference in seconds.

Healthcare & Parenting March 2026
AI filled two medical intake forms while I was in transit

Our newborn (2.5 weeks old) was referred to an occupational therapist for a suspected tongue-tie and infant feeding concerns. Two intake forms stood between us and her first appointment — a quick interest form and a full 10-section clinical intake. I handled both while doing other things, spending about 2 minutes of total effort.

Result: ~35–40 minutes of form work ondensed to ~2 minutes of effort. Form 1 saved ~10 minutes (found, filled, and submitted while I was in transit). Form 2 saved ~25–30 minutes on a complex multi-section clinical intake. The appointment is booked.
What happened, step by step
1

Form 1 — Initial Interest Form: I was on my way to the pediatrician when I asked AI to fill out the clinic’s online interest form. AI found the form link directly from the referral email, then filled all 13 questions automatically — caregiver info, baby details, insurance, therapy type, feeding concerns, and referral source. I reviewed the pre-filled answers and AI submitted. Zero manual input from me.

2

Form 2 — Full Clinical Intake: A follow-up email from the clinic contained a link to their patient portal. AI found the link, navigated to it, and triggered an SMS verification code. I read the 6-digit code aloud — about 2 seconds. AI logged in automatically.

3

AI then worked through all 10 sections of the clinical intake without pausing: child info, doctor and referral details, caregiver information, birth history, home environment, medical history, prior treatment history, insurance details, credit card authorization, and legal agreements. The only moments I stepped in were to confirm our home address and complete the signature field.

4

Every other field — 100% of the clinical content — was filled by AI drawing from context it already had: our baby’s birth details, our insurance information, the referring pediatrician’s name, and her feeding and developmental history. Nothing had to be looked up or typed by hand.

The takeaway: new-parent admin is relentless. Between pediatrician visits, insurance calls, and specialist referrals, paperwork piles up fast — especially when you’re running on no sleep. AI handled the intake process end-to-end — finding the forms, logging into the portal, and navigating every section. I just showed up.

Finance & Home March 2026
AI negotiated my internet bill down — while I was doing something else

My internet bill had quietly crept up to $80/month — $30 above the $50/month I originally signed up for on a 1 GB plan. I handed it off to AI. It opened a live chat, declined three counter-offers, and got the original price back — while I made coffee.

$80/month → $50/month. $30 off every month — $360 saved per year. Three offers. AI said no to all of them.
What happened, step by step
1

Opened the live chat and asked for loyalty discounts. AI identified as a long-term customer, asked what retention offers were available, and was transferred to a retention specialist.

2

Laid out the situation. Current plan: 1 GB at $80/month. Original signup rate: $50/month. Competitors offering comparable plans for less. Switching was explicitly on the table.

3

The retention specialist laid out the situation. The existing $20 loyalty credit expires March 28 and can’t be renewed — and there’s no discount available on the current 1 GB plan. The only offer on the table: upgrade to a 2 GB plan at $70/month (vs. the current 1 GB at ~$80–95/month). More speed, lower bill — but a plan change. AI identified three paths forward: accept the 2 GB upgrade at $70/month, push harder for $70 or less on the existing 1 GB, or ask directly if $70 could apply to the 1 GB plan. I asked AI to push for the original price on the 1GB plan.

4

AI pushed for the original rate. “I’ve been a customer for years. The plan I signed up for was 1 GB at $50/month. I’d like that restored.” The specialist agreed after playing a 10 min waiting game.

5

Confirmed the rate in the chat transcript. $50/month restored on the original 1 GB plan. Total time: 25 minutes.

Retention specialists lead with upgrades because most people get distracted. AI doesn’t. It remembered the goal: restore the original plan, not buy more.

The takeaway: most people overpay on recurring bills not because they can’t negotiate, but because they never get around to it. AI removed the only real obstacle — the time and friction of actually doing it. The savings are real; the effort was zero.
